Why Organic Cotton Will Eventually Pave The Way For Zero-Virgin Cotton

Projekt Indigo

JANUARY 30, 2021

We briefly touched upon this thought in one of our previous articles if organic cotton is as reassuring as it sounds. Now though the world is screaming #safehands, we don’t mind getting our hands a bit dirty (virtually) with some facts about the real impact of organic cotton on the environment.

The Genuine Good

Organic cotton often gets the popular vote for its many facets of friendliness. From the complete refusal of GMOs (genetically modified organisms) at the birth stage, avoiding fertilisers and pesticides for growth, saving water and energy till the end, it has a pretty healthy lifespan. While many of these life choices have their own downsides, the most surefire change is seen in the water impact. Organic cotton directly reduces the impact on our natural water resources as the crops are 80% rain-fed. Considering it uses no chemicals, it causes 98% less water pollution as well.

The Flip-side

Organic cotton often gets the popular vote for its many facets of friendliness. From the complete refusal of GMOs (genetically modified organisms) at the birth stage, avoiding fertilisers and pesticides for growth, saving water and energy till the end, it has a pretty healthy lifespan. While many of these life choices have their own downsides, the most surefire change is seen in the water impact. Organic cotton directly reduces the impact on our natural water resources as the crops are 80% rain-fed. Considering it uses no chemicals, it causes 98% less water pollution as well.

“Virgin cotton, no matter how harmless, inevitably adds to the problem of over-production in a world which accumulates almost 5 times the waste every year.”

While organic cotton depends mostly on rain-water, the same factor limits the area and timeline of its production. Most countries are dependent on importing organic cotton because their natural climate doesn’t support its cultivation. Moreover, the lower yield per hectare leads to increased pressure on land and other energy sources. Unless farming units support cotton production with the usage of completely renewable energy, they won’t be able to reduce the environmental impact greatly in the long run. Not to be progressively pessimistic, but in several instances, organic cotton meets chemical dyes and fabric enhancers. This renders the final garment not-so-organic. And when these garments end up in landfills as biodegradable waste, they stand a greater chance of polluting the land.

A More Sustainable Alternative

An alternative path lies in zero-virgin cotton, which can be regenerated completely from recycled cotton yarns. Zero-virgin directly counters the flip sides of organic cotton, reducing the impact on fresh natural resources. And while its production process does involve chemicals, manufacturing units can reuse these chemicals as well. A comprehensive comparison of the impact created by both these materials is sure to end in favour of regenerated cotton. But very few manufacturing setups across the world are aware of this fairly new concept. Therefore it’s still in the process of meeting logistic perfection to emerge as a clear, better choice.
